Tarot Journaling: A Practice You Can Return To

Published 20 July 2026

Simple prompts, a three-line format, and how to record the echoes that follow. A journal that grows deeper with each page.

Tarot journaling means writing down what a card showed you in your own words. Not the guidebook meaning—your meaning. The pattern you noticed. The question the card raised. Over time, you create a record of your reflections and can trace the threads that return again and again.

The Three-Line Format

1

The Card

Write down which card you drew and what stood out in the image. One sentence. "The Hermit, and I noticed his lantern is small but bright."

2

Your Reading

Write what the card means to you right now. Not the universal meaning—your meaning. One to three sentences. "This card feels like I'm in a season of quiet learning. I need solitude, not because I'm sad, but because I'm listening."

3

The Echoes

Write one real-world thing you noticed after the reading that echoed the card's meaning. Come back and add to this line later. "Three days later, I chose a quiet morning instead of a coffee date. That felt right."

Five Journaling Prompts

Prompt One: What is this card mirroring?

Write what you see in the card that matches something true about your life right now. This is the mirror work: not prediction, but recognition.

Example: "The Eight of Pentacles shows someone practicing a craft. I've been practicing my meditation practice, and it's starting to feel less forced. This card says: yes, you're building something real."

Prompt Two: What is the card inviting me to notice?

The card isn't telling you what to do. It's pointing. What is it pointing at that you might have missed?

Example: "The Ace of Cups is new emotion, new possibility. I've been numb for a while. This card invites me to notice: when did I last feel something? What opened that door?"

Prompt Three: If this card were advice, what would it say?

Not fortune-telling. Gentle advice from a trusted friend. What would this card whisper to you?

Example: "The Temperance card feels like advice: 'You don't have to choose one or the other. Find the balance. Both can be true.'"

Prompt Four: What question does this card raise?

Sometimes a card doesn't answer—it asks. Write the question that came up when you saw it.

Example: "The Two of Swords shows someone blindfolded. My question: 'What am I choosing not to see? And is that choice still serving me?'"

Prompt Five: What will I pay attention to this week?

Based on this card, what will you notice? What sign will you look for? This is the daily practice that makes tarot journaling come alive.

Example: "The Six of Cups brings gentleness and memory. This week, I'll notice moments of small kindness. I'll pay attention to what my younger self would have wanted to hear."

How Journaling Deepens Your Practice

The magic of tarot journaling isn't in the writing itself. It's in the returning. Six months from now, you'll flip back through your journal and see: "Oh, that pattern again." Or: "I've moved on from that question." Or: "I'm still learning the same lesson." The journal becomes your mirror to yourself.

Every time you write, you're translating the card's language into your own. You're making tarot personal. You're saying: this means something to me. And that's the whole point.
